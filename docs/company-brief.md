**Founder:** Mike Stein

**Site:** idatlas.org
**Tagline:** Mapping Objectives to Measurable Results
**Model:** Apprenticeship-style instructional design agency
**Founded:** 2024 (contracting-agency-with-apprentices model; preceded by one-on-one tutoring and coaching work). Two-year anniversary: May 2026.

---

## At a glance

ID Atlas is a boutique instructional design agency that delivers custom eLearning, serious games, and accessibility work for higher ed, small businesses, and nonprofits. What makes it structurally unusual is the apprenticeship model: rather than hiring senior IDs to scale up margins, Mike intentionally hires people earlier in their careers and pays them to learn on real client work. The mentorship is the product philosophy, not overhead. The agency funds itself through a mix of stable contracts, agency-overflow subcontracting, and inbound Reddit and blog leads, and is in early development on two adjacent assets: an open-source authoring tool and ChatTrain, a conversational micro-training platform connected to Mike's doctoral research.

## Origin

ID Atlas grew out of two parallel frustrations.

First, Mike's previous role at Howard Community College included faculty consultation, classroom observation, and one-on-one coaching, which he genuinely enjoyed. When he transitioned to fully remote eLearning work, that coaching dimension dropped almost entirely. The agency was, in part, a way to rebuild it.

Second, Mike rejects the ID bootcamp economy. The standard pitch ($6,000 to $7,000 for a few weeks of training, a portfolio, and the promise of a six-figure work-from-home career) leaves graduates without legitimate credentials, with shallow technical skill, and with unrealistic expectations of the job market.

The seed of the model came earlier and smaller: a Reddit DM from a transitioning teacher who wanted Articulate Storyline tutoring. The person had pedagogical instincts but no production reps and very little money. Mike took it on, and that arrangement set the template: pay people to learn on real work instead of charging them to learn on fake projects.

ID Atlas formally launched in 2024 when Mike evolved the model from one-on-one tutoring into a contracting agency with subcontracted apprentices. The clearest articulation of the agency's premise in writing comes from a March 2024 DM exchange with Cherry, a B2B marketer who had posted in r/instructionaldesign about switching careers. In that DM Mike committed, in writing, that his goal was not to keep apprentices working with him but to help them transition into the field, or out of it, as soon as they knew which. Cherry apprenticed for two years and then graduated sideways out of the field to become a travel agent, making her the first full-arc proof case for the model. ID Atlas hit its two-year anniversary in May 2026.

## Business model: apprenticeship agency

The core operating pattern is a scaffolded handoff:

1. Mike builds the first module of a project to establish the design pattern, voice, and technical approach.
2. He hands an apprentice the second module along with a template, his version as reference, or both.
3. The apprentice builds it.
4. Mike does QA, edits, and polish before the deliverable ships to the client.
5. Mike shows the apprentice the diff so they learn from the revision, not just from feedback.

Apprentices invoice monthly for hours worked. Mike absorbs the QA and edit time as part of the markup. Where finishing requires substantial Mike-level production work, he bills those hours separately on the client side. The arrangement is honest with apprentices about what they're getting (mentorship and real-project experience) and honest with clients about what they're getting (Mike's quality bar on the final deliverable, with subcontracted production underneath).

This model has structural implications worth being explicit about:
- Margins are tighter than a typical agency would tolerate, because Mike eats time.
- Throughput is bounded by Mike's QA capacity, not by apprentice headcount.
- Scaling by hiring more senior IDs would technically improve margins but would dissolve the thing that makes the agency distinctive.

## Economics

**Client pricing:**
- Hourly is the default because most clients prefer it.
- Flat-rate is offered and used where it fits.
- Retainers are not currently in use but Mike is open to them.

**Apprentice pay:**
- Standard rate is $25/hr.
- Invoiced monthly, paid for hours worked.
- Adjusted up over time as quality grows (see the Filipino apprentice's $7 to $25 arc).

**Margin pattern on a typical subcontracted hour:**
- Mike's rate to clients typically runs $60–70/hr, with a floor of $50/hr.
- Apprentice receives $25/hr.
- Mike absorbs QA, edits, and polish in the spread.
- Where finishing requires substantial additional Mike-level effort, those hours are billed separately to the client.
- Typical per-hour margin on apprentice production: $35–45. Floor: $25 when rates flex down.

The structural takeaway is that margin is real and workable, but Mike eats unbilled QA time, so the model functions more like a teaching practice that pays than like a leverage-maximizing agency.

## Products in development

### Open-source authoring tool

**Status:** Active development, not yet production-ready, currently on the bench while doctoral work takes priority.

**Thesis:** The dominant authoring tools (Articulate 360 in particular) charge approximately $1,800/seat/year, which for a five-person team is close to $10,000 annually. In an era where AI can generate substantial parts of an eLearning course, that pricing increasingly looks like a tax on production rather than a fair exchange for value. Mike's belief is that a comparable or better tool can be built and released openly, freeing teams from vendor lock-in.

**Possible structure:** Mike is weighing running the tool as a separate nonprofit rather than under ID Atlas. Nonprofit framing would open donations and grants and would also avoid the optics tension of competing with a vendor (Articulate) that Mike publicly benchmarks. The decision is unresolved.

**Personal stake:** Mike is mostly building the tool he himself wants to use. Open-sourcing is the philosophical commitment; everything else flows from that.

### ChatTrain

**Status:** Fully working and functional. Currently piloting.

**What it is:** A conversational micro-training platform connected to Mike's doctoral research (a phishing-reporting Level 3 comparison study).

**Positioning:** ChatTrain is explicitly not being pursued as a standalone software product. Mike does not want to be a software company running 24/7 support for thousands of license-holders. Instead, ChatTrain is a service-line tool inside ID Atlas: a deliverable Mike and his apprentices can use to build conversational micro-training for clients. The go-to-market motion is that ID Atlas will build a client's phishing micro-training and deliver it through ChatTrain, not that the client should license ChatTrain from ID Atlas. Mike is open to revisiting this if a serious commercial path emerges, but the default stance is service-line, not software company.

## Strategic tensions and open questions

These are the live trade-offs visible from the outside that Mike has signaled awareness of:

1. **The measurement promise.** The tagline foregrounds measurable results, but in practice most engagements ship-and-done because clients don't want to fund post-launch measurement. The honest options are to dial the tagline back or to productize a lightweight measurement deliverable cheap enough that clients can't refuse it.

2. **Authoring tool: nonprofit or commercial.** The open-source commitment is fixed. The wrapper around it is not. Nonprofit unlocks grants and clean optics; commercial is faster and more flexible but creates direct-competitor friction with Articulate while Field Notes publicly benchmarks them.

3. **Webinars and vendor advisory as a real service line.** The first paid engagement, with an authoring tool company, is suggestive. This revenue shape is very different from per-hour course development and could become a meaningful second leg if pursued deliberately.

4. **Doctorate-vs-agency time allocation.** Field Notes, Discord, ID Case Files, and apprentice cadence all suffer when doctoral work spikes. A more explicit plan for what hibernates and what stays on would reduce the silent-erosion problem.

5. **The dormant Discord.** A community that exists in name but not in activity is worse than no community. Either reactivate or formally retire it.

6. **Scaling apprenticeships through international hiring.** The Filipino apprentice is a proof of concept. Brazil has been tried with mixed results. Whether to invest in finding more high-potential international apprentices, or to keep headcount small and high-touch, is a live question with real implications for the agency's identity.

7. **ChatTrain stance is firm but worth periodic re-examination.** Service-line-only is the right default given Mike's bandwidth and aversion to running a software company. If a strong commercial signal emerges during piloting (a paying customer who wants to license, a partner who wants to white-label), the question of whether to spin ChatTrain out should be reopened deliberately rather than absorbed by default.
